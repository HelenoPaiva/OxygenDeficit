Oxygen Deficit Calculator

Updated for a workflow where the anesthesia machine shows inspired and expired O₂ as vol% rather than PETO₂ in mmHg. Enter inspired O₂, expired O₂, the machine pressure reading you use as Vm / barometric pressure, and the app converts the gas fractions into oxygen partial pressures for teaching.

Conversion logic

FiO₂ fraction = inspired O₂ vol% / 100
Expired PO₂ ≈ (expired O₂ vol% / 100) × (PB − 47)
PAO₂ = FiO₂ × (PB − 47) − PETCO₂ / R
Oxygen deficit = expired PO₂ − estimated PaO₂
A–a gradient = PAO₂ − estimated PaO₂
• This is a teaching simplification.
• If your gas module reports concentration in vol%, converting with pressure is the key extra step.
• Expired O₂–derived PO₂ and calculated PAO₂ are related, but they are not the same thing.

Teaching interpretation

This version starts from machine-displayed oxygen concentrations in vol% and converts them into partial pressure before comparing them with the estimated arterial oxygen level.

Expired PO₂ ≈ 0.140 × (760 − 47) = 99.8 mmHg
Oxygen deficit = 99.8 − 80.0 = 19.8 mmHg
PAO₂ = 0.210 × (760 − 47) − 40 / 0.8 = 99.7 mmHg
A–a gradient = 99.7 − 80.0 = 19.7 mmHg

In the classroom, this makes the logic explicit: if the monitor gives you vol%, you first convert concentration into pressure, then compare alveolar-side oxygen with arterial-side oxygen.

Important teaching caveat: this app assumes a simplified conversion from gas concentration to partial pressure and uses PETCO₂ as a surrogate for PaCO₂. It is meant for physiology teaching, not bedside decision-making. Depending on how the module dries the sample gas and displays concentration, real measured values can differ modestly from ideal humidified alveolar values.
